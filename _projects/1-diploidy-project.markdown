Diploidy (along with other forms of multiploidy) is ubiquitous among complex organisms, but it is still unclear which evolutionary forces drove the convergence upon this particular genetic architecture. Understanding diploidy assists not only in understanding the evolution of genomes, but also in implementing algorithms which adapt to their problem space. If biological diploidy provides adaptive advantages over haploidy, then diploid algorithms may exhibit higher relative performance.

Related to this issue are issues of recombination and crossover. Biological models show that these processes are essential for the evolution of diploidy, but how should they be implemented in digital models? Can they be made emergent properties of digital systems, as in biological ones? 

This work is being conducted with Clifford Bohm and Cara Reedy.
